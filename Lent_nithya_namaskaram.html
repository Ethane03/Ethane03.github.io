Foreword

There is a misconception that the season of Lent is only a season of fasting. But it should be seen as a season that awakens the imagination of practices both mental and physical. As food is needed for the body, and air for life, prayer is necessary for the nourishment of the soul. The prayer lives of the church fathers make clear that the early church found power through a life of prayer, while passing through the midst of persecution. The set of prayers during Hasha week (Holy Week) and other fasts recall to us this treasure. Abraham Melpan Achan formed the daily prayer book from such a treasure chest of prayers and gave it as a gift to the spirituality of the Mar Thoma church. In earlier times, this order of worship was used for daily prayer. The treasure was distributed in prayers throughout the various hours of the day during Hasha week. Because the prayers are out of use and often overlooked in this season, is desirable to reorganize our prayer lives toward the season of Lent.

This order of worship for Lent, formed from the treasure mine of the prayers of the fathers under the leadership of our Joseph Mar Barnabus Episcopa Thirumeni, will be helpful to forming an imaginative prayer live. Through its use, the treasure that the Eastern church fathers have given us in prayers and songs will be seen. I pray that the use of this order of prayer will help you move from the inclination of recent times to only see spirituality in emotion, and to grasp the value in a spiritual life through meditation and humility.

I hope that the church members might use this order of prayer and discover its value. Much gratitude to everyone involved in the composition of this liturgy. This order of prayer is permitted for use in our houses and prayer groups.

  • Joseph Mar Thoma Metropolitan

 

 

Note on the Translation

This translation was made primarily from the Malayalam version, Valiya Nombu Namaskarakramam. That in turn was done from the Syriac. Some meaning of the original Syriac was likely lost during the two translations. This is obvious in some of the discrepancies between my translation and the translation used by the Malankara Orthodox Church. For example, this line of a Bo’utho from the Sunday prayers:

Mar Thoma

The evil one tried to fight but was put to shame When he spread his net, his hand became weak and frail

Orthodox

The evil one tried to fight but was put to shame Christ ensnared him when He raised His hand in weakness

Most likely, the Orthodox translation is more faithful to the original Syriac. However, I have followed the Mar Thoma wording in order to maintain congruency between the Malayalam and English versions of the liturgy.

This first line in that quotation also highlights my debt to the Malankara Orthodox Church. The Orthodox Church produced a beautiful translation of their Lenten Prayers, upon which I had little hope of improving. Many of the verses and stanzas in this book are shared with the Orthodox liturgy. For those I have chosen to use the translation done by the Orthodox Church, with some minor changes.

The Malayalam language has a variety of forms that cannot be accurately differentiated in English. How can English express the formality and complexity of words like vandipuuthaakename  or Kelkaanidayaakaathirikkendathinu? I have attempted to match the tone of the Malayalam version, using Thee and Thou as well as some of the more archaic English verbs like art when appropriate. For similar reasons, the Psalms are taken from the Coverdale Psalter, except for the Kukylon (Pethgome) which are adapted to fit the verse structure.

 

 

Explanation of the Liturgy

Most of the material in the common prayers is familiar to members of the Mar Thoma Church.

The Exaltation is used in the Divine worship and in the Thuyaba service before Holy Qurbana

The Qauma is common to nearly all Christian liturgies. Quama means “standing” in Syriac, as the prayer is meant to be said standing. It is known as the Trisagion (thrice-holy) in western Christianity. The part “Crucified for us” was added during the miaphysite/diaphysite controversy to affirm that God Himself was crucified.

The Shayana Namaskaram is an ancient hymn written by St. Ephrem (c. 306 - 379).

The Nicene creed is shared with much of the rest of Christianity. The English translation used in the Mar Thoma church is unique in that is says “The third day He rose again by his Father’s Holy will”. The original Nicene creed says in accordance with the Scriptures. The Malankara Orthodox and Jacobite churches uses of his own will. The Malayalam version in the Marthoma as well as the Orthodox uses thiruvishtam or thiruhitham (The holy Will) with no mention of the Father.  If these differences are only due to translation errors, they ought to be rectified to match the original version.

The rest of the liturgy is divided into days. Each day is structured like so:

  • Opening prayer
  • Psalm
  • Enyana (Responsory Hymn)
  • Promeon
  • Sedra
  • A number of Qolos (Hymns)
  • Quqlion (Psalm)
  • Eqba (Stanza)
  • Another Qolo (Hymn)
  • Bo’uthos (Prayer Hymn)
  • Concluding prayer
  • Common Prayers, sermon, scripture readings, etc.

Qolo means voice or hymn in Syriac. Qolos (Qole) are meditative hymns. They are more often directed to the singer than to God, encouraging us to fix our minds on God, Scripture, the Church, the Saints, etc.

Bo’uthos (Bo’uthe) are intercessory hymns. These are always directed to God. They are essentially sung prayers, making appeals to God. There are two kinds of these hymns: Bo’utho of St. Jacob of Serugh, which have 12 syllable lines, and Bo’utho of St. Ephrem of Nisbis, which have 7 syllable lines. These saints are not the authors of most of the hymns that bear their name. But because they were the first to use their respective syllabic structures, all later hymns in that structure are dedicated to them. There are also Bo’uthe of St. Balai, with 5-syllable structures, but these do not appear in this book.

Eqbas are single stanza excepts from longer hymns.

Quqlion are quotations from the Psalms, set to verse. Quqlion comes from the Greek work Kuklos, meaning cycle. In the liturgy it means a cycle of hymns.  The portion of the Qurbana liturgy beginning with “As for us, our days are like grass…” is a Quqlion. The true name for these verses is Pethgomo. But, since a Quqlion always starts with a Pethgomo, the two words have become conflated over time.

A Promeon is a short preface to a Sedra. It usually describes the virtues of Jesus. It is preceded by Staumenkalos kurielaison and ends with …At this time of prayer, on all holy days and times and hours, and all the days of our lives, thou art worthy of honor and praise and worship. Hosho vab’kula suban l’olmeen.

Sedra means “Array” in Syriac. They are long arrays of prayers, consisting of dozens of short requests.

A few phrases are left in Syriac or Greek, their meanings are below:

Barek’mar             Bless, O Lord

Staumenkalos             Stand in reverence
